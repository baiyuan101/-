### 自由——慎辨性与关怀的演化

我们所要强调的关怀伦理和一些其他的规范伦理学（如中国的儒家伦理）最大的不同在于，关怀伦理不会固守任何固有的价值秩序，它要求关怀者和被关怀者随着关系的变化随时调整对于关怀的认知。例如在人的一生当中与自己的父母之间的关怀关系会逐渐发生变化。在幼年和少年时代，他主要是一个被关怀者，他所需要做的是学会如何给予父母以正确的反馈。而随着他的年龄增大，他会逐渐成为一个关怀者。在父母的暮年时段，他就需要出演一个关怀者的角色，而此时父母也面临着角色转变的过程。这不像是强调“父为子纲”的儒家伦理对关系重心的一惯性的强调。也不像美德伦理强调如社会责任、诚信等个人品质的恒长性。

关怀伦理并不是这些价值的反对者，它也同意在现时代正常的人类社会的恰当情景中应该遵守这些价值。只是在很多情景中，这些价值难免有些不合时宜。例如在一个被你有所怀疑的一个骗局情景中，你是否依然要对对方保持诚信呢？你不会告诉他你的一些重要信息，以保护自己的信息安全。因为，此时你们根本就没有建立恰当的关系。此外，学者帕梅拉·迈耶（Pamela Meyer）发现越是紧密的关系当中，欺骗的次数反而越多。这并不难想象，在一对热恋的情人之间恐怕绝大多数甜言蜜语都是有水分的。即便是在亲密的朋友之间，人们也往往会“虚与委蛇”地称赞对方穿的衣服好看等等。由此可见，人们并非在任何时刻都是某些价值的恪守者。朋友间“友情互吹”的谎言，确切地说是一种“非典型谎言”，因为处于关系中的双方都知道这是一个谎言，并且理解这个谎言的用意。举这样的一个例子，不是我们想强调谎言是在这些情形下维持关系的必要条件，而是我们希望就此说明人们在很多情形下为了维持关系而选择放弃了某些价值，这是在任何时代和地区都在发生着的事实。

所以问题的核心应该在于如何在增进关系的前提下维持某种价值，抑或说将代表着不同价值的关系理顺，而不是一味地坚持某种价值或价值秩序。显然，这是一个更加深入的话题，我不认为简单地通过将女性主义伦理学（早期关怀伦理的主要价值来源来自女性主义伦理学）的某些价值加持在关怀伦理之上就可以解决这个问题。所以我们在此无法给出一个充分必要的条件来说明这一点——来说明根据何种普遍性的原则能一劳永逸地处理所有道德情境中的问题。关怀伦理的实践者应该放弃这种古典的道德理想主义的想法，至少在内容上不再追求完美的道德。关怀伦理实践更多的是一个探索性的过程，我们需要提供一个能够给这个过程持续的提供活性的能力，同时提供一个矫正机制。这两者将直接与我们的关怀教育要的培养目标相关。

正如我们已经说过的，关系的变化是一个演化过程。当孩子从家庭进入学校之后，他们首先需要面对的就是关怀关系的演化，他们当中的相当一部分从一个几乎是自我中心的被关怀者成为了一个平等社会中的一员。他们必须学会处理同学之间的关系。而友谊意味着一种全新的关怀关系，这是一种相对平衡、对等的关怀与被关怀的关系。一个孩子只有适应了同时作为一个恰当的关怀者和被关怀者，他才能适应友谊。从家庭进入学校，是人一生中关键的一步，这是因为对相当多一部分人来说，只有进入学校他才真正有机会完成自己对关系认知的转换。对于在中国的许多独生子女小孩子而言，他们很容易形成一种以家庭自然关系为纽带的自我中心主义，而家长们也很少会有意识地将自己的孩子视作一个伦理客体，而非血亲对象。对于一个伦理客体而言，责任和义务有一定的对等性。孩子作为被关怀者需要学会如何对大人的关怀进行反应，而不是一味地接受。要完成这一点，意味着孩子必须对于和父母之间构建关系的概念有清楚地认知。显然，孩子无法通过书本认识这一点，而需要通过平时的生活教导来认知这一点。

我的一个晚辈，曾经有一次因为他的父亲为了不让他玩智能手机而设置屏幕解锁密码而大发脾气。他气愤地质问他的父亲：“是你重要还是我重要？”。他的潜台词是他比他的父亲更重要，所以他的父亲必须服从他的利益。这一现象的原因显然是家庭溺爱的结果。在这个时候，我们需要做的不是粗暴地训斥和惩罚，而是必须告诉他：他的父亲和他一样重要。我作为这个孩子信赖的长辈，成功地对他进行了一次教导。我需要让孩子知道的是，他的自我中心地位不是无限的，甚至在他和他的父亲的关系中也不是无限的。孩子需要知道，在这件事情中他为什么会感到意外，智能手机属于他的父亲，而父亲有权处理这个手机的使用权，只有父亲认为必要的时刻他才能够使用这个手机。在很多育儿的经验当中都有类似的条款，即不能无限地满足孩子的需求，必须让他知道并且清楚哪些需求是无理的。在这个例子中，孩子必须知道，没有限制地使用手机对他自己有可能存在伤害，也会妨碍父亲的正常生活。

需要明白的是，教导的目的不是树立新的规范和禁区，让孩子无条件遵守，而是在训练他依靠概念来思考自己和他们的关系，并且能够在不同的关系中自由地转换。我们都知道，偏见是会伴随人的一生，而且将持续地同建立更广泛的关怀关系相对抗的一种心理特征。而真正的自由的表征之一，就应该是能够对自己身上已经存在以及可能获得的偏见的自觉。这很难、确切地说是无法做到的。这看上去是一个令人沮丧的结论，但它实际上可能并非我们想象的那样。

正如我们在“关怀——以概念为中介的关系”一节中所提到的，联结关系的是不同的概念。我们并不想涉及太多的认知心理学的内容，不过有必要明确，即概念不是集合所对应的内涵，而是一种概念网络的表征物。人类在认知世界的时候并非完全依赖分析性的思维，在构建关系的时候尤其如此。例如“鸟”同时有可能和“游泳”和“飞翔”挂钩——如果你想到的是“海鸥”的话，你自然会同意这个关系，但如果你想到的是“鹰”的话，你似乎就会对这个关系产生排斥——这取决于你的经验积累，以及你依赖这些积累对于未来的直觉。并不是说，你只见过“鹰”，就一定不认为“鸟”和“游泳”没有关系，你当然可以想象或许有一种禽类是可以在水面生活——尽管这个概率比较小。对于我们来说，自由就是对于概念和关系的结构持有开放性的态度。只有这样，在种族歧视盛行的年代，才有可能让人认知黑人一样可以有高尚的品格，卓越的学习能力和智慧，并进一步应当被等同于白人对待。

但从另外一个方面来说，我们需要避免的是偏见，却无法通过切断所有偏见的动机来达成这一点。我们的认知世界原本就是通过概念联结而成的，任何一个概念网络都不可能平衡地与其他所有概念产生联系，它必须有特定的边界——这是概念的固化或类别化。类别化是人类简化认知世界的基本方式，为了提高效率我们不得不对许多事情“贴标签”。正如我们在前文中强调过的，关怀伦理不应该自诩为不偏不倚的关系处理原则，我们必然会犯错。问题的关键，在于灵活而持久的纠错机制。而这个纠错机制的基础就是——永远对反面证据采取开放性的态度。

### Freedom and the Evolution of Care

The version of care ethics I want to defend differs from more rigid normative traditions in one central respect: it does not assume that one fixed value-order should be preserved unchanged in every circumstance. Care ethics asks both carer and cared-for to adjust their understanding of care as the relation itself changes. Consider the relation between parents and children. In childhood and adolescence, a person is mainly a cared-for subject and must learn how to respond properly to care. Later, that same person may gradually become the carer, especially when parents enter old age. The relation changes; the pattern of care changes with it. This is quite unlike traditions that insist on preserving one stable hierarchy within the relation.

That does not mean that care ethics opposes values such as honesty, responsibility, or social trust. On the contrary, it agrees that in the ordinary and healthy circumstances of contemporary social life those values matter and should usually be respected. The problem is that they are not always applicable in the same way. If you suspect that you are dealing with a scam, should you still disclose private information in the name of honesty? Obviously not. At that point, no proper relation has yet been established. I am not saying that deception is a positive ideal, still less that every lie preserves a relationship. I mean only that human beings often relax one value in order to preserve another relational good. Lovers flatter one another. Friends sometimes praise clothes they do not really admire. Such speech can be insincere in a narrow sense, yet both sides often understand perfectly well what kind of social work it is doing.

So the real problem is not how to cling to one value at all costs. It is how to preserve value while also sustaining or rightly ordering relationships that carry different values. No single universal principle can solve every case in advance. That is why care ethics, as I understand it, must remain exploratory. We need not a final formula but a durable capacity for correction.

This is also why I speak of the evolution of care. When children move from the family into school, the first thing they confront is not merely new knowledge but a new ecology of relationships. Many of them move from being almost wholly self-centred recipients of care into a more equal social world. Friendship introduces a new kind of caring relation, one in which a person must learn how to be both a proper carer and a proper cared-for person. For many people, school is the first setting in which this transformation can actually take place.

In China, many only children easily develop a form of egocentrism tied closely to family relations. Parents often do not consciously treat their children as ethical subjects, but simply as blood relations. Yet once a child is approached as an ethical subject, responsibility and obligation can no longer remain entirely one-sided. The child, as the cared-for person, has to learn how to respond to care rather than merely absorb it. That cannot be learned from books alone. It has to be learned through ordinary, repeated episodes of life together.

I once saw this very clearly in a younger relative of mine. His father set a password on a smartphone so that the child could not use it without permission, and the child exploded in anger. He demanded of his father: "Who matters more, you or me?" The hidden premise was obvious: I matter more, so my interests should decide the question. This was not a moment for crude punishment. It was a moment for education. What had to be explained was simple but important: his father mattered no less than he did; the phone belonged to the father; and the father therefore had the right to regulate its use. The educational point was not to create a new taboo and demand blind obedience. It was to train the child to think conceptually about relations and to move more freely within them.

That freedom matters because prejudice accompanies us throughout life and continually resists the widening of caring relations. One sign of genuine freedom is some degree of awareness of the prejudices we already possess and of those we may acquire later. This can never be complete. In that sense, there is something discouraging about the conclusion. But it is not quite as bleak as it may sound.

As I argued in the previous section, relationships are mediated by concepts. I do not want to wander too deeply into cognitive psychology here, but one point matters: a concept is not merely a neat definition corresponding to a set. It is part of a network. Human beings do not cognize the world only through pure analysis. This is especially true when they build relationships. A concept like "bird," for instance, may connect in one person's experience with both flying and swimming if the relevant image is a seagull, while another person may reject the connection because the image in mind is an eagle. Experience, expectation, and intuition all shape what appears natural to us. Freedom, in this sense, means maintaining openness toward the structure of concepts and relationships. Without that kind of openness, people would not have been able, in times of racial discrimination, to recognize that Black people are equally capable of dignity, intelligence, and moral excellence, and therefore equally entitled to just treatment.

At the same time, we cannot eliminate prejudice simply by trying to eliminate all motives for prejudice. Human beings necessarily organize the world through concepts, and any conceptual network has boundaries. Categorization is one of the basic ways in which we simplify an overwhelmingly complex world. We label things because doing so increases efficiency. The question, then, is not how to abolish categories. It is how to prevent categories from hardening into unquestioned errors. Since care ethics cannot honestly present itself as a perfectly impartial method for handling every relation, mistakes are inevitable. What matters is a flexible and enduring mechanism of correction. And the basis of that mechanism is a lasting openness to counter-evidence.

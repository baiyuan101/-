## Literal Translation

In November 2016, the popularity of an article titled "Luo Yixiao, You Stop Right There" rose sharply in WeChat Moments. It was an emotionally charged piece written by the father of a little girl suffering from leukemia and urgently awaiting treatment. There is no doubt that it stirred the sympathy of hundreds of thousands of Chinese internet users, so much so that I, who originally had nothing to do with the matter, was also drawn into this tidal wave of feeling and contributed a small amount of money through the article's tip function. Later, however, the whole story turned abruptly downward. Luo Yixiao's father, Luo Er, was accused of making improper profit through online operation and the marketing of emotion, because the proceeds of his "fund-raising" had already reached more than 2.6 million yuan, and some people discovered that his family's financial situation was not nearly as desperate as had been suggested and that they were able to pay for their daughter's medical treatment. For a time, Luo Er changed from a pitiable father "selling his writing to save his daughter" into a "swindler" accused of manipulative sentimentalism, to the point that he fell into a crisis of public credibility and none of his later explanations were trusted.

For me, what was worth thinking about in this case was this: why Luo Yixiao? There were so many little girls with leukemia in need of help, so why was she the one who received attention? This happened because people's empathy was, by some means, nurtured into a concentrated surge and focus of feeling. What lay behind this? Perhaps a remark by the eighteenth-century British philosopher David Hume can provide us with an explanation: "There is no passion in human minds but what has reference to some person or other, besides ourselves, and some real object of love or hatred." [1] In other words, any feeling concerning human beings must be a feeling "about some particular person"; more precisely, it is a feeling about some particular feature of that person. This is not actually difficult to understand. Even Hegel, that great master of German classical speculation, when he saw Napoleon riding into Berlin, compared him to "the world spirit on horseback" - one of the most abstract concepts in his philosophy. He was concretizing or personifying his cognitive object so as to reach some genuinely perceptible level of vividness. This kind of apprehension is first-personal and, in a certain sense, "subjective"; perhaps that is precisely one of the common ways in which human beings cognize the world. Clearly, Luo Er made use of this, whether deliberately or not. In that article, his talent for vivid language was displayed to the full, making people, myself included, feel deeply both Luo Yixiao's loveliness and her pathos, and then turn toward pity for a father terrified of losing his beloved daughter. Social media magnified this emotion to an astonishing degree, thereby producing a striking imbalance in public attention. [1]

According to Hume's theory, human sympathy cannot be extended without limit. Suppose, for example, that the same thing had happened not to a child but to an animal - an Asian elephant from Thailand. It might not have received nearly so much sympathy from Chinese readers. For them, elephants can be seen only rarely, in zoos or on television; not many people would form an overly strong and concrete emotional identification with an elephant. In other words, sympathy of this sort, which depends on vivid concretization, is shaped by the circles of everyday life. Those circles can be drawn in different ways: race, nationality, profession, gender, or even political conviction. According to Jeremy Rifkin in *The Empathic Civilization*, every concept that helps one form a certain self-consciousness also helps divide up one's "belonging." These belongings are the basis on which compassion and empathy are built. In other words, for those outside my own sphere of belonging, the intensity of our empathic feeling is unlikely to be equally strong.

This theory of empathy will not be accepted by everyone. The ethical principle of effective altruism proposed by the contemporary ethicist Peter Singer seems not to require this sort of empathy, which he treats as emotional empathy, as its basis. In his view, it consists only in empathic concern and personal distress triggered by another person's emotions. These emotions are not themselves "right" or "wrong"; it is simply that they do not appear to belong to those effective altruists whose goal is to pursue the greatest altruistic good for the greatest number. To some degree, effective altruists are no different from "cold-blooded" utilitarians. They do not alter what they regard as the more rational charitable principle because of a report with strong emotional force or an event they have personally witnessed. For example, the same amount of money could be used either to help Luo Er or to help five equally endangered famine victims in Africa. Even though they may invest less empathic concern in the latter, they are still willing to donate the money to them, because doing so produces a greater altruistic effect. Experiments have shown that utilitarians, and likewise effective altruists, display a lower level of affective empathy in certain situations. So they seem more capable of making a "rational" judgment about how one ought to act altruistically. [2] [3]

For effective altruists, then, does ethical action no longer need empathy as a necessary condition? If empathy is defined only as affective empathy, perhaps that is indeed so. But if empathy is defined as "being able to think from another person's point of view," or as "being able to draw information from another person's response in order to change one's own decision," then effective altruists still need this rational mechanism to assist their decision-making. Yet this kind of empathy is no longer an "emotion." Those who hold such a view will no longer respond to others' needs according to the degree of their pity. Perhaps the subjective effect of another person's need becomes one important objective parameter in calculation. For example, in Eiichiro Oda's famous manga *One Piece*, the protagonist Luffy does not help those who merely need help as a matter of fact; rather, he is more inclined to help those who urgently want to change their own situation and actively seek his assistance. Luffy is of course not an effective altruist, but effective altruists may perhaps learn something from his way of judging. In other words, effective altruism can direct help toward those individuals whose positive response is likely to be greater, because in that case the utility of the help will obviously be maximized. This sort of empathy seems to carry no "subjective colour." It ought to be a third-person standard that anyone can rationally recognize and employ, and we shall see that it more readily assists in constructing a normative moral philosophy.

Evaluating effective altruism is not our purpose. But if people who think in this way merely regard empathy as a strategic instrument, that is something we do not wish to see. Although it is undesirable simply to indulge one's affective empathy, it also seems impossible to eliminate it altogether. We can explain this on the basis of scientific facts. Numan's model of maternal caregiving is a theory used to explain the care behaviour of mammals toward their young, and it emphasizes the decisive role of hormones such as oxytocin (OT) and dopamine (DA) in the arousal of empathy. Research has shown that this model also applies to human beings. Mascaro and others conducted a comparative experiment on sixty fathers of children aged one to three and found that, compared with seeing unfamiliar children, seeing their own children produced a higher level of activation in the ventral tegmental area, an important source of dopamine release. Dopamine, as is well known, is associated with reward and has been found to correlate strongly with parental behaviour. It is therefore evident that affective empathy is an innate function rooted in our biology. Obviously, we cannot suppress everyone's dopamine release through drugs and thereby weaken their empathy. That would be inhumane and unscientific. If human beings are not to be changed by means of biological enhancement, then any proposal we make must begin from accepting human affective empathy. [4] [5]

Another problem with effective altruism is that it may also fall into the kind of difficulty frequently encountered by many forms of normative ethics: namely, taking a conclusion that is not in fact drawn from an idealized condition and promoting it as though it were an ideal conclusion or norm. We cannot determine whether an effective altruist has truly achieved "impartiality." In our view, that is very difficult, and there is no need to overemphasize it.

Accordingly, for us, perhaps a "both-and" concept of empathy is the more reasonable one. In other words, we acknowledge both the emotional side of empathy and the importance of treating empathy as an element in rational decision-making. This means:

1. We admit that empathy is "limited." It is real, and for that very reason it is naturally susceptible to inducement by many factors; nor is it the basis of some boundless universal love. But we also believe that empathy, as an emotion, can be corrected by education, behavioural science, and the capacity for self-reflection. In other words, we believe that a relatively "better" empathy is something worth pursuing. We do not advocate the use of biological enhancement, such as drugs or surgery, to alter a person's level of empathy. In this sense, we acknowledge the subjective reality and effectiveness of first-person empathy, but we do not think that, simply because it is natural, it therefore ought not to be changed. Rather, we believe that empathy education is necessary, and, as things stand, is almost the only way to help human beings improve their empathic condition.

2. We do not intend to pursue a universal normative conception of moral responsibility from a third-person standpoint. Any cultural community with a shared character will have its own moral contents that carry empathy. Thus, the difficult question for moral philosophers - how broad consensus can be established - naturally troubles us as well. Frankly speaking, we neither expect nor recommend the establishment of a strong consensus or strong moral norms that hold everywhere and for all. But this does not mean that we believe different cultures of empathy cannot establish relations of mutual understanding with one another. Those relations may for now be fragile, but that does not mean they cannot become stronger. The philosophical aim of life-care education lies precisely here: it is not a kind of knowledge education about particular doctrines or behavioural rules, but a practical education that helps human beings learn how to build such relations. We call the basis of this motive force a form of "second-person" empathy.

By the "second person" we mean a point of departure that understands the spiritual or mental contents of the human world in terms of interactive interpersonal relations. Given this special perspective, its theoretical investigation also draws from some particular fields. In cognitive psychology there is a theoretical path known as theory of mind. This influential approach makes a distinctive assumption about the growth of the human mind. It holds that the mental development of an individual human being is grounded in that person's "reading" of other minds. Of course, theorists differ in their understanding of mind-reading. Some scholars think that human beings can simulate a model of another person's mental state on the basis of their own subjective state, building empathy for others by moving "from oneself to others"; this is the so-called simulation theory (ST). Others maintain that human beings possess a rational capacity to construct hypothetical "theories" about other people's mental states, gradually accumulating evidence through later interaction and revising those theories; this is known as theory theory (TT). At present, influential accounts of theory of mind often combine elements from both sides in order to explain the basis of human mind-reading. Our notion of "second-person" empathy likewise presupposes both theories. If a provisional definition is needed, we would state it as follows:

The development of second-person empathy is a process in which mental simulation from self to other provides the material for constructing conjectural theories about other minds, and in which one gradually refines and corrects both one's way of simulating and one's way of designing such "theories."

We believe that in many cases the initial theory one forms about the psychological state of an unfamiliar other is indeed activated by moving from oneself to others. Adolescents in particular still lack a sufficiently rich and mature set of "theories" within their cognitive systems, and for that reason their empathy often displays a degree of egocentrism. This is because they still lack enough theory-theory type cognitive operations and therefore cannot accurately grasp the more complex and widely shared modes of understanding common among larger groups of people. Nor do they know to what extent their own subjective state regarding some person or thing possesses generality. A philosophy of care education based on second-person empathy should therefore aim to help adolescents more smoothly acquire a normal empathic cognitive capacity. If they possess this capacity, then their value system will naturally be influenced by the community to which they belong. But which empathic cultural clusters within human society they will ultimately enter is not something we can control. In this sense, it is difficult for us to judge a person's level of second-person empathy on the basis of that person's values or moral views alone. Second-person empathy has no final state. The only thing we can say with relative confidence is that a person progressing through second-person empathy in a relatively normal way will not easily depart from the empathic cultural community in which that person is situated, while at the same time maintaining openness toward unfamiliar new cultures or individuals.

From an objective point of view, second-person empathy is a "limited consensus." It does not recognize any permanent agreement regarding some mental content or moral commandment, and it also opposes excessive insistence on a person's exclusive subjective feelings or wishes. Social customs and common sense tell us that there is a basis for mutual understanding among human beings, but also the latent danger of disagreement. A constructive empathy ought to help social development by keeping disagreement within a "safe and beneficial" limit, yet no individual can determine exactly where that limit lies. Clearly, this is not something that any one person's effort can settle, just as no one, however hard he tries, can guarantee that a beloved person will respond to his love as he hopes. Yet human beings are not wholly without resources. At least three important intellectual achievements may be regarded as highly beneficial to the attainment of a "safe and beneficial limited consensus."

The first is rational procedures of communication and thought; rationality here does not mean formal logic.

The second is philosophical reflection concerned with preserving the original flexibility of concepts.

The third is positive action.

The first and second can be seen as belonging to the education of critical thinking, whereas the third derives from positive psychology. In the next section we shall try to explain the definition and extension of care.

## Reading-Friendly Version

In November 2016, a WeChat article titled "Luo Yixiao, Stop Right There" spread rapidly across Chinese social media. Written by the father of a young girl with leukemia, it was meant to move readers emotionally, and it did. Hundreds of thousands of people responded with sympathy. I did too. Although I had no personal connection to the family, I was caught up in the emotional force of the piece and donated a small amount of money through the tipping function. Later, however, the story turned sharply. Luo Yixiao's father, Luo Er, was accused of using online publicity and emotional marketing for improper gain. The money raised had already exceeded 2.6 million yuan, and some people claimed that the family's financial situation was not as desperate as the article had implied. In a short time, Luo Er was recast in public opinion from a tragic father "selling his writing to save his daughter" into a manipulative fraud. Once public trust collapsed, his later explanations no longer carried much weight.

What interested me most was a different question: why Luo Yixiao? Why did this child, rather than so many other children with leukemia who also needed help, become the object of collective concern? My answer is that empathy was shaped, intensified, and focused in a particular way. Here David Hume gives us a useful starting point. Hume argues that our feelings do not attach themselves to "humanity in general" in the abstract; they attach themselves to particular people, particular qualities, and particular relations to us. [1] Even when we think we are responding to "human suffering," what usually moves us is some concrete and perceptible person.

That is not hard to understand. Even Hegel, the philosopher of abstraction, described Napoleon entering Berlin as "the world spirit on horseback." He gave one of the most abstract concepts in philosophy a strikingly concrete image. Human beings often understand things by personifying them or making them vivid. That kind of apprehension is first-personal and, in an important sense, subjective. Luo Er made use of exactly this mechanism. His writing made readers, myself included, feel the loveliness and vulnerability of Luo Yixiao and then, through her, feel pity for a father afraid of losing his daughter. Social media amplified that response to an astonishing degree, and the result was a clear imbalance in public attention. [1]

On Hume's view, sympathy does not extend indefinitely. If the same thing had happened not to a child but to an Asian elephant in Thailand, the response from Chinese readers would probably have been much weaker. Most people encounter elephants only rarely, in zoos or on television, and so they are less likely to form a strong emotional identification with one. Sympathy that depends on vividness is shaped by the circles within which we live. Those circles may be organised by race, nationality, profession, gender, or political belief. Jeremy Rifkin makes a related point in *The Empathic Civilization*: the concepts through which we form a sense of self also define our sense of belonging. Those structures of belonging become the social basis of both compassion and empathy. It follows that our empathic intensity is usually weaker toward those outside our own circles.

Not everyone accepts this picture. Peter Singer's ideal of effective altruism seems not to require this kind of emotional empathy at all. On his account, empathy often amounts only to empathic concern or personal distress triggered by another person's situation. Those emotions are not, in themselves, morally authoritative. They may even distract us from actions that would produce the greatest good for the greatest number. In that sense, effective altruists can look like "cold-blooded" utilitarians. They do not revise their principles simply because a story is emotionally powerful. The same sum of money, for example, might be used either to help Luo Er or to save five equally desperate people in Africa. Even if a donor feels less emotional identification with the latter, the money may still go there because that choice produces greater altruistic effect. Some experiments suggest that utilitarians, and effective altruists in similar situations, show lower levels of affective empathy in certain scenarios. If so, then human beings may indeed be able to regulate empathy and keep it within the boundaries set by reason. [2] [3]

Does this mean that ethical action no longer needs empathy? Only if we define empathy narrowly as emotional sympathy. If empathy also means the ability to think from another person's point of view, or to treat another person's response as information that should reshape one's own decision, then effective altruists still depend on empathy, though no longer in a purely affective form. Empathy becomes a rational capacity for taking others seriously. The intensity of my feeling is no longer the only measure; another person's need and response can become part of the objective parameters of judgment.

That is why I argue for a both-and concept of empathy. We should acknowledge the emotional side of empathy while also treating empathy as part of rational decision-making. On the one hand, empathy is limited, uneven, and easily guided or manipulated. On the other hand, it can be improved through education, behavioural science, and self-reflection. A better form of empathy is therefore worth pursuing. What I reject is the idea that we should change empathic capacity through biological enhancement, whether by drugs or surgery. Numan's work on maternal caregiving and Mascaro's study of fathers both suggest that care is tied to evolved neurobiological systems involving oxytocin and dopamine. [4] [5] Emotional empathy is not an accidental extra; it is part of our inheritance. If we are not prepared to redesign human beings biologically, then any realistic programme must begin from that fact.

Effective altruism also faces a more familiar problem in normative ethics. It is too easy to treat a conclusion that has not really been derived under ideal conditions as if it were a universally valid norm. We cannot be sure that any effective altruist has truly achieved perfect impartiality. In my view, that ideal is both extremely difficult to realise and too easy to overstate.

For that reason, a more plausible concept of empathy is one that tries to hold both sides together. First, empathy is limited and shaped by circumstance, but it can still be educated, corrected, and refined. That is why empathy education matters. At present it may be the only realistic way to improve the human empathic condition without turning to biological manipulation. Second, we should not expect to derive a universally binding moral responsibility from a purely third-person standpoint. Different cultural communities will always carry different moral contents through which empathy is expressed. The question that troubles moral philosophers - how broad consensus is possible - therefore troubles us as well. I do not expect, and do not recommend, that we try to impose a single strong moral consensus that holds everywhere. What matters instead is learning how different empathic communities can enter into relations of mutual understanding, however fragile those relations may initially be.

This, I think, is where life-care education should begin. It is not simply a matter of teaching rules or transmitting moral propositions. It is a practical form of education aimed at helping people build those relations of mutual intelligibility. The motive force behind that project is what I call second-person empathy.

By "second person" I mean a way of approaching human mental and moral life through interactive relations between persons. Theory of mind research is useful here. Some theorists explain our understanding of others through simulation: we model another person's mental state by projecting from our own. Others emphasise theory-building: we develop hypotheses about other minds and revise them in light of experience. Contemporary work often combines both approaches. My own idea of second-person empathy does the same. A provisional definition would be this: second-person empathy develops through a process in which self-to-other simulation provides the raw material for conjectures about other minds, while those conjectures are continuously revised and refined through interaction.

This matters especially in education. Young people often begin by understanding others through themselves. Because they do not yet possess a sufficiently rich and mature stock of theories about other people, their empathy can easily remain egocentric. They may not yet know how far their own feelings or interpretations are generalisable, or how much their personal standpoint differs from the more complex patterns of understanding shared by broader communities. A philosophy of care education grounded in second-person empathy should therefore help adolescents acquire a more stable and recognisably normal capacity for empathy. Once they develop that capacity, their value system will naturally be shaped by the community to which they belong. But which empathic cultures they ultimately inhabit is not something educators can fully control. Second-person empathy has no final state. Still, a person who is developing in a relatively healthy way through this process will not easily detach from the empathic culture of their community, and will also remain open to unfamiliar people and new forms of life.

From an objective point of view, second-person empathy amounts to a limited consensus. It rejects the fantasy of permanent agreement on one fixed mental content or moral law, but it also rejects excessive insistence on merely private feeling or will. Social customs and common sense tell us both that mutual understanding is possible and that disagreement is inevitable. Constructive empathy should help social life keep disagreement within a range that is safe and beneficial, even though no individual can determine exactly where that range lies. No one can guarantee, simply through private effort, that another person will respond as one hopes. Yet human beings are not helpless here. At least three intellectual resources can help us move toward this limited but productive consensus: rational practices of communication and thought, philosophical reflection that preserves conceptual flexibility, and positive action. The first two belong broadly to critical thinking; the third connects to positive psychology. In the next section I turn to the meaning and scope of care itself.

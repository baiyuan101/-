# Chapter 8. The Educational Philosophy of Care

## Empathy as a Second-Person Standpoint

In November 2016, a WeChat article titled "Luo Yixiao, Stop Right There" spread rapidly across Chinese social media. Written by the father of a young girl with leukemia, it was meant to move readers emotionally, and it did. Hundreds of thousands of people responded with sympathy. I did too. Although I had no personal connection to the family, I was caught up in the emotional force of the piece and donated a small amount of money through the tipping function. Later, however, the story turned sharply. Luo Yixiao's father, Luo Er, was accused of using online publicity and emotional marketing for improper gain. The money raised had already exceeded 2.6 million yuan, and some people claimed that the family's financial situation was not as desperate as the article had implied. In a short time, Luo Er was recast in public opinion from a tragic father "selling his writing to save his daughter" into a manipulative fraud. Once public trust collapsed, his later explanations no longer carried much weight.

What interested me most was a different question: why Luo Yixiao? Why did this child, rather than so many other children with leukemia who also needed help, become the object of collective concern? My answer is that empathy was shaped, intensified, and focused in a particular way. Here David Hume gives us a useful starting point. Hume argues that our feelings do not attach themselves to "humanity in general" in the abstract; they attach themselves to particular people, particular qualities, and particular relations to us.[^1] Even when we think we are responding to "human suffering," what usually moves us is some concrete and perceptible person.

That is not hard to understand. Even Hegel, the philosopher of abstraction, described Napoleon entering Berlin as "the world spirit on horseback." He gave one of the most abstract concepts in philosophy a strikingly concrete image. Human beings often understand things by personifying them or making them vivid. That kind of apprehension is first-personal and, in an important sense, subjective. Luo Er made use of exactly this mechanism. His writing made readers, myself included, feel the loveliness and vulnerability of Luo Yixiao and then, through her, feel pity for a father afraid of losing his daughter. Social media amplified that response to an astonishing degree, and the result was a clear imbalance in public attention.[^1]

On Hume's view, sympathy does not extend indefinitely. If the same thing had happened not to a child but to an Asian elephant in Thailand, the response from Chinese readers would probably have been much weaker. Most people encounter elephants only rarely, in zoos or on television, and so they are less likely to form a strong emotional identification with one. Sympathy that depends on vividness is shaped by the circles within which we live. Those circles may be organised by race, nationality, profession, gender, or political belief. Jeremy Rifkin makes a related point in *The Empathic Civilization*: the concepts through which we form a sense of self also define our sense of belonging. Those structures of belonging become the social basis of both compassion and empathy. It follows that our empathic intensity is usually weaker toward those outside our own circles.

Not everyone accepts this picture. Peter Singer's ideal of effective altruism seems not to require this kind of emotional empathy at all. On his account, empathy often amounts only to empathic concern or personal distress triggered by another person's situation. Those emotions are not, in themselves, morally authoritative. They may even distract us from actions that would produce the greatest good for the greatest number. In that sense, effective altruists can look like "cold-blooded" utilitarians. They do not revise their principles simply because a story is emotionally powerful. The same sum of money, for example, might be used either to help Luo Er or to save five equally desperate people in Africa. Even if a donor feels less emotional identification with the latter, the money may still go there because that choice produces greater altruistic effect. Some experiments suggest that utilitarians, and effective altruists in similar situations, show lower levels of affective empathy in certain scenarios. If so, then human beings may indeed be able to regulate empathy and keep it within the boundaries set by reason.[^2] [^3]

Does this mean that ethical action no longer needs empathy? Only if we define empathy narrowly as emotional sympathy. If empathy also means the ability to think from another person's point of view, or to treat another person's response as information that should reshape one's own decision, then effective altruists still depend on empathy, though no longer in a purely affective form. Empathy becomes a rational capacity for taking others seriously. The intensity of my feeling is no longer the only measure; another person's need and response can become part of the objective parameters of judgment.

That is why I argue for a both-and concept of empathy. We should acknowledge the emotional side of empathy while also treating empathy as part of rational decision-making. On the one hand, empathy is limited, uneven, and easily guided or manipulated. On the other hand, it can be improved through education, behavioural science, and self-reflection. A better form of empathy is therefore worth pursuing. What I reject is the idea that we should change empathic capacity through biological enhancement, whether by drugs or surgery. Numan's work on maternal caregiving and Mascaro's study of fathers both suggest that care is tied to evolved neurobiological systems involving oxytocin and dopamine.[^4] [^5] Emotional empathy is not an accidental extra; it is part of our inheritance. If we are not prepared to redesign human beings biologically, then any realistic programme must begin from that fact.

Effective altruism also faces a more familiar problem in normative ethics. It is too easy to treat a conclusion that has not really been derived under ideal conditions as if it were a universally valid norm. We cannot be sure that any effective altruist has truly achieved perfect impartiality. In my view, that ideal is both extremely difficult to realise and too easy to overstate.

For that reason, a more plausible concept of empathy is one that tries to hold both sides together. First, empathy is limited and shaped by circumstance, but it can still be educated, corrected, and refined. That is why empathy education matters. At present it may be the only realistic way to improve the human empathic condition without turning to biological manipulation. Second, we should not expect to derive a universally binding moral responsibility from a purely third-person standpoint. Different cultural communities will always carry different moral contents through which empathy is expressed. The question that troubles moral philosophers - how broad consensus is possible - therefore troubles us as well. I do not expect, and do not recommend, that we try to impose a single strong moral consensus that holds everywhere. What matters instead is learning how different empathic communities can enter into relations of mutual understanding, however fragile those relations may initially be.

This, I think, is where life-care education should begin. It is not simply a matter of teaching rules or transmitting moral propositions. It is a practical form of education aimed at helping people build those relations of mutual intelligibility. The motive force behind that project is what I call second-person empathy.

By "second person" I mean a way of approaching human mental and moral life through interactive relations between persons. Theory of mind research is useful here. Some theorists explain our understanding of others through simulation: we model another person's mental state by projecting from our own. Others emphasise theory-building: we develop hypotheses about other minds and revise them in light of experience. Contemporary work often combines both approaches. My own idea of second-person empathy does the same. A provisional definition would be this: second-person empathy develops through a process in which self-to-other simulation provides the raw material for conjectures about other minds, while those conjectures are continuously revised and refined through interaction.

This matters especially in education. Young people often begin by understanding others through themselves. Because they do not yet possess a sufficiently rich and mature stock of theories about other people, their empathy can easily remain egocentric. They may not yet know how far their own feelings or interpretations are generalisable, or how much their personal standpoint differs from the more complex patterns of understanding shared by broader communities. A philosophy of care education grounded in second-person empathy should therefore help adolescents acquire a more stable and recognisably normal capacity for empathy. Once they develop that capacity, their value system will naturally be shaped by the community to which they belong. But which empathic cultures they ultimately inhabit is not something educators can fully control. Second-person empathy has no final state. Still, a person who is developing in a relatively healthy way through this process will not easily detach from the empathic culture of their community, and will also remain open to unfamiliar people and new forms of life.

From an objective point of view, second-person empathy amounts to a limited consensus. It rejects the fantasy of permanent agreement on one fixed mental content or moral law, but it also rejects excessive insistence on merely private feeling or will. Social customs and common sense tell us both that mutual understanding is possible and that disagreement is inevitable. Constructive empathy should help social life keep disagreement within a range that is safe and beneficial, even though no individual can determine exactly where that range lies. No one can guarantee, simply through private effort, that another person will respond as one hopes. Yet human beings are not helpless here. At least three intellectual resources can help us move toward this limited but productive consensus: rational practices of communication and thought, philosophical reflection that preserves conceptual flexibility, and positive action. The first two belong broadly to critical thinking; the third connects to positive psychology. In the next section I turn to the meaning and scope of care itself.

## Care as a Concept-Mediated Relationship

Readers familiar with Nel Noddings will notice that my account of care has much in common with hers. I also agree with her on one basic point: care should be understood first as a relationship, not as an abstract rule and not as a virtue floating free of relationships. Even so, I want to say more about the inner structure of care, because that structure affects both the scope of care and the educational purpose of care education.

Early Noddings distinguishes between two forms of care: caring for and caring about. Caring for is thicker and more concrete. The care that parents give their children, or that partners give one another, belongs here. In many cases it rests on natural relations, such as kinship, though not always; it can also arise in relations built later, such as comradeship or friendship. Caring about, by contrast, is thinner and more fragile. It is often attached to social roles, public concern, or duties of service. It can still matter, but it often feels as though there is a layer left between the two sides, and that is why it does not always generate deep trust.[^6]

I can explain the difference through one of my own experiences. Years ago, while travelling in Thailand, I joined a jungle adventure course that my adventure-loving wife had booked for us. Participants moved along simple cable routes between tall trees under the guidance of instructors. It was exciting, but I am mildly afraid of heights, so I could not enjoy it in quite the way other people did. When we reached a rapid-descent segment, the kind often featured in survival-adventure television shows, I felt that I really could not handle the speed. I asked the instructor whether he could lower me more slowly. He was a young man, and at first he looked surprised; perhaps, in his eyes, this was one of the most enjoyable parts of the course. But once he saw that I was serious, he helped me down carefully and then paid extra attention to me during the rest of the activity.

What kind of relation was that? By Noddings's standards, it would be hard to say that the instructor and I had already entered into a full relation of caring for. We were strangers. The course leaders had also already told participants that they were entitled to make requests according to their own needs. So, on the surface, our interaction may seem to belong only to the level of caring about. Even so, I do not think that is a reason to deny its positive value. His concern helped me, and I experienced it as a real and beneficial form of care.

Noddings sometimes describes caring about and caring for as if they stand in sharp contrast. Dickens's Mrs Jellyby in *Bleak House* is a familiar example: she pours her energy into caring about distant strangers while neglecting those closest to her. In a case like that, her concern for distant others becomes morally distorted because she has, in some degree, lost the ability to receive the people around her as full human beings rather than as background figures.[^7] That is an important warning. But I still do not think the difference between the two should be treated as absolute.

My own inclination is to say that caring for can sometimes be understood as a fuller development of caring about rather than as a completely separate moral species. The key question is whether the relation begins to generate trust through action and response. In my own case, I did not expect the instructor's concern to turn into any rich or lasting form of care. I certainly did not expect him to understand my fear in depth. Yet he did something important: he recognized that my fear was real for someone like me, and he adjusted his conduct accordingly.

For that reason, I describe care as a concept-mediated relationship. Full caring for appears when the carer can receive the other person as a whole individual rather than merely as the occupant of some role under a concept. That is not easy. It becomes relatively easier, I think, in at least two situations. The first is when a person has not yet been flattened into rigid, one-dimensional social roles and has previously lived within healthy relations of care. The Little Prince is a good image here. His care for the rose is simple, direct, and free of calculation. It is almost pre-social. The second situation is when one person has come to understand another person's circumstances in sufficiently rich detail. That fuller form of care often appears in parent-child relations or in durable marriages. My relation with the Thai instructor belonged to neither category.

Even so, I still remember his help very clearly. We did not have time for extended conversation. In fact, I can no longer remember his face in detail. Yet I remember the structure of the relation. We both recognized that we stood in different positions with regard to fear. Neither of us needed to calculate the exact degree of difference. What mattered was that he was willing to widen the space within which my response could count as intelligible. If he had helped me only in a cold, mechanical way, simply because the job required it, I would not have understood his action as care. I would only have thought: he did his job. The difference lies in the mediating relation itself.

This is also why I do not want to oppose care as relationship to care as practice. If care is truly a relationship, it still has to be enacted. Caring about cannot remain empty speech. It must contribute, in however modest a way, to the strengthening of trust between the carer and the cared-for, and that trust has to become recognizable through action, response, and sometimes even through words.

At the same time, I do not want to define concern too thinly. If concern is treated merely as a simple role-based relation attached to a simple social norm, then it no longer matches what I mean by care. No real human relationship can be captured entirely by such a flat description. If my instructor had offered only a cold, role-driven assistance, I would not have regarded it as concern in the richer sense. I would only have registered an efficient service. What I am looking for instead is something grounded in second-person empathy.

Borrowing Stephen Darwall's vocabulary, such a relation involves claims, reasons, and responsibilities.[^8] Of course, our prior arrangement was not meaningless. The fact that I was a participant and he was an instructor gave me a legitimate starting point from which to make a request, just as a waiter has some reason to hear a diner's request. But that starting point alone could not yet generate care. The decisive step was that he had to grasp my reason. He had to understand why I was asking. Only then could his role-based responsibility begin to acquire the content of care. In ordinary Chinese, we would call this a matter of "using one's own heart to understand another's."

To be clear, I do not think he felt any deep sympathy for me. From the point of view of a young jungle-sports instructor, I probably looked timid and weak. But that is exactly the point. He did not need to feel as I felt. He only needed to recognize that such fear exists as a real possibility for some people. He had probably met travellers like me before. Experience had already taught him that this kind of response pattern exists, and that if he responded cooperatively he would usually receive a corresponding response in return. Once that pattern is reinforced, concern gradually becomes part of the content of professional responsibility itself.

That is one reason feedback loops matter so much in caring relations. In many cases, what looks like a caring act does not generate any clear feedback at all. The money one gives to a beggar on the street, or the donation one makes to an online fund-raising campaign on impulse, may certainly have some effect, but that effect is not normally returned to the giver in any visible way. Natural sympathy alone may therefore be insufficient. A reasonably reliable feedback loop matters because it helps us judge whether the relation is actually functioning as a caring relation. Concern without effective feedback should be treated with caution.[^9]

Only once we see this can we understand another important feature of care: caring relations cannot generate fully impartial obligations. A carer cannot be expected to treat every possible object of care in exactly the same way, because care is limited by the conditions of relation itself. We can, of course, care for animals in distant places. But we can do so only if some effective line of relation can really be formed. This is not a declaration of human exceptionalism. It is a more practical claim: care becomes real when relation becomes effective.

Elephant Nature Park near Chiang Mai offers a good example. Each year it receives volunteers from around the world to help care for rescued elephants. I once visited there as a tourist. What impressed me was the way the park combined public education, limited tourism, and genuine welfare work. Visitors were not encouraged simply to consume the elephants as entertainment. Instead, they were told about the suffering the elephants had endured before rescue, shown how they were treated and cared for, and allowed limited interaction, such as washing them under supervision. Volunteers, by contrast, stayed much longer and built a more sustained relation. Experiences like this often make people more attentive to animal welfare afterwards. Stronger contact makes concern more concrete.

Still, not everyone who visits a place like that becomes an animal advocate. In my view, at least two qualities are needed if a person is to widen the range of care in a stable way. The first is a good will that genuinely wants to build healthy relations. The second is a capacity to revise one's own concepts and views with a measure of freedom. It is to that second issue that I now turn.

## Freedom and the Evolution of Care

The version of care ethics I want to defend differs from more rigid normative traditions in one central respect: it does not assume that one fixed value-order should be preserved unchanged in every circumstance. Care ethics asks both carer and cared-for to adjust their understanding of care as the relation itself changes. Consider the relation between parents and children. In childhood and adolescence, a person is mainly a cared-for subject and must learn how to respond properly to care. Later, that same person may gradually become the carer, especially when parents enter old age. The relation changes; the pattern of care changes with it. This is quite unlike traditions that insist on preserving one stable hierarchy within the relation.

That does not mean that care ethics opposes values such as honesty, responsibility, or social trust. On the contrary, it agrees that in the ordinary and healthy circumstances of contemporary social life those values matter and should usually be respected. The problem is that they are not always applicable in the same way. If you suspect that you are dealing with a scam, should you still disclose private information in the name of honesty? Obviously not. At that point, no proper relation has yet been established. I am not saying that deception is a positive ideal, still less that every lie preserves a relationship. I mean only that human beings often relax one value in order to preserve another relational good. Lovers flatter one another. Friends sometimes praise clothes they do not really admire. Such speech can be insincere in a narrow sense, yet both sides often understand perfectly well what kind of social work it is doing.

So the real problem is not how to cling to one value at all costs. It is how to preserve value while also sustaining or rightly ordering relationships that carry different values. No single universal principle can solve every case in advance. That is why care ethics, as I understand it, must remain exploratory. We need not a final formula but a durable capacity for correction.

This is also why I speak of the evolution of care. When children move from the family into school, the first thing they confront is not merely new knowledge but a new ecology of relationships. Many of them move from being almost wholly self-centred recipients of care into a more equal social world. Friendship introduces a new kind of caring relation, one in which a person must learn how to be both a proper carer and a proper cared-for person. For many people, school is the first setting in which this transformation can actually take place.

In China, many only children easily develop a form of egocentrism tied closely to family relations. Parents often do not consciously treat their children as ethical subjects, but simply as blood relations. Yet once a child is approached as an ethical subject, responsibility and obligation can no longer remain entirely one-sided. The child, as the cared-for person, has to learn how to respond to care rather than merely absorb it. That cannot be learned from books alone. It has to be learned through ordinary, repeated episodes of life together.

I once saw this very clearly in a younger relative of mine. His father set a password on a smartphone so that the child could not use it without permission, and the child exploded in anger. He demanded of his father: "Who matters more, you or me?" The hidden premise was obvious: I matter more, so my interests should decide the question. This was not a moment for crude punishment. It was a moment for education. What had to be explained was simple but important: his father mattered no less than he did; the phone belonged to the father; and the father therefore had the right to regulate its use. The educational point was not to create a new taboo and demand blind obedience. It was to train the child to think conceptually about relations and to move more freely within them.

That freedom matters because prejudice accompanies us throughout life and continually resists the widening of caring relations. One sign of genuine freedom is some degree of awareness of the prejudices we already possess and of those we may acquire later. This can never be complete. In that sense, there is something discouraging about the conclusion. But it is not quite as bleak as it may sound.

As I argued in the previous section, relationships are mediated by concepts. I do not want to wander too deeply into cognitive psychology here, but one point matters: a concept is not merely a neat definition corresponding to a set. It is part of a network. Human beings do not cognize the world only through pure analysis. This is especially true when they build relationships. A concept like "bird," for instance, may connect in one person's experience with both flying and swimming if the relevant image is a seagull, while another person may reject the connection because the image in mind is an eagle. Experience, expectation, and intuition all shape what appears natural to us. Freedom, in this sense, means maintaining openness toward the structure of concepts and relationships. Without that kind of openness, people would not have been able, in times of racial discrimination, to recognize that Black people are equally capable of dignity, intelligence, and moral excellence, and therefore equally entitled to just treatment.

At the same time, we cannot eliminate prejudice simply by trying to eliminate all motives for prejudice. Human beings necessarily organize the world through concepts, and any conceptual network has boundaries. Categorization is one of the basic ways in which we simplify an overwhelmingly complex world. We label things because doing so increases efficiency. The question, then, is not how to abolish categories. It is how to prevent categories from hardening into unquestioned errors. Since care ethics cannot honestly present itself as a perfectly impartial method for handling every relation, mistakes are inevitable. What matters is a flexible and enduring mechanism of correction. And the basis of that mechanism is a lasting openness to counter-evidence.

## Adaptive Virtue and the Aim of Care Education

Every form of moral education eventually faces the same problem: how should it respond to social change and shifting value systems? In the contemporary world, cultural conservatism often triggers two equally unhelpful reactions. Some people idealize the past without qualification. Others want to demolish everything inherited from tradition. Care ethics, as I understand it, does neither. It does not grant tradition any sacred status. Traditions are hard to change because they store accumulated experience and stabilize social relations, not because they are beyond criticism.

The real issue is where change should begin. What is the proper point of intervention in moral education? I do not think the answer is straightforward moral enhancement, understood as the strengthening of some fixed value. That can sometimes serve as a temporary expedient, but I do not think it should define our educational goal. My deeper concern is with overly aggressive programmes of value-shaping. Education should still help people develop, but development ought to mean helping them live better with themselves, form healthier relations with the world, and remain alert to changing circumstances. That is why I describe the final aim of care education as the cultivation of adaptive virtue.

Carol Gilligan's *In a Different Voice* is especially useful here. Gilligan famously challenged Lawrence Kohlberg's stage theory of moral development. For Kohlberg, justice is the highest aim of moral cognition. The more mature the person, the more likely that person is to reason through universal principles. Gilligan argues that this framework treats male reasoning as the norm and therefore makes women appear morally underdeveloped. My own proposal goes one step further: both male and female moral development can be reinterpreted within a broader moral framework centred on relation.[^11]

Kohlberg's famous Heinz dilemma makes the disagreement vivid. Heinz cannot afford the medicine that would save his wife. Should he steal it? In Gilligan's interviews, the eleven-year-old boy Jake answers with confidence: yes, because life is more important than money. His reasoning is orderly and principle-driven. Amy, by contrast, seems hesitant. She says Heinz should not steal the medicine, but should instead keep looking for another way, perhaps by borrowing money or finding help elsewhere. Even when the dilemma is tightened, Amy continues to resist the direct appeal to theft. Kohlberg reads this as a lower stage of moral development. Gilligan reads it differently. Amy is not confused. She is reasoning from a different centre. She keeps returning to the relation between Heinz and his wife and to the risk that either theft or inaction may damage that relation beyond repair.[^12] [^13] [^14]

This is why Gilligan can speak of a different moral voice. In her account, women do not simply fail to reach the male standard; rather, they often reason through a structure that is more attentive to relation, dependency, and responsibility as lived between persons. In that framework, moral development does not move simply from selfishness to universal principle. It also moves through stages in which relation first appears as something external and binding, then becomes something more balanced and reflectively inhabited.

Her interviews about abortion show the same pattern. Some women choose abortion from the standpoint of survival or immediate self-interest. Others feel trapped by the expectations of family, partner, or society. A literary example appears in *Revolutionary Road*, where April Wheeler is torn between her own aspirations and the pressures of marriage, pregnancy, and social life.[^15] The real philosophical question is not simply whether abortion is chosen or refused. It is what kind of judgment-structure lies behind the decision. Is the person trying to balance self and relation? Or is she simply surrendering herself to the relation, or sacrificing the relation entirely to herself?

At this point my own view diverges from Gilligan's. I do not think men and women stand on wholly different moral foundations. Even Kohlberg's stages can be re-read through the concept of relation.[^17] Boys, too, reason relationally. The difference is that the relevant relation is often more abstract. Jake is not thinking chiefly about the concrete bond between Heinz and his wife; he is thinking about the relation between competing principles, such as legality and the priority of life. What Gilligan calls a masculine standard is, in my view, an overemphasis on conceptual relation at the expense of lived interpersonal relation.[^18] That is why Jake's answer should not be described as morally higher. It is simply structured differently.

Once this is clear, a broader point follows. Because relations are mediated by concepts, people will differ in how they adapt to social life, cultural settings, and moral demands. Care ethics should respect this plurality. But respect for plurality cannot mean educational passivity. If education is to do any real work at all, it still has to say something about what is good, what is right, and why. The difference is that it should display its reasons rather than merely impose them, preserve variety in how those reasons are interpreted, and cultivate disciplined habits of inquiry rather than obedience for its own sake.

That last point also matters because moral development is always affected by many non-moral factors. Anyone who reads social psychology seriously will soon see that some social mechanisms are especially likely to damage moral judgment, while others can be positively used in education. Those findings, in my view, provide some of the most important material for what I call critical thinking education. It is to that topic that I now turn.

## Critical Thinking and the Education of Care

In this section I want to address three questions: what critical thinking is, how critical thinking education can be integrated with care education, and which teaching principles matter most in school settings.

### Critical Thinking

In Chinese contexts, the standard translation of "critical thinking" is literal enough, but it also produces a predictable misunderstanding. Many people hear "critical" and assume it means simple negation, as though the point were merely to attack, reject, or refute. That misunderstanding does not belong only to casual readers. It appears even among students taking courses in the subject. For that reason, I sometimes prefer a more neutral phrase, something closer to "careful discernment," especially when critical thinking is being brought into conversation with care education. In that setting, the aggressive, concept-first flavour of "critical" can easily become misleading.

Critical thinking has to be understood in two senses. First, it is a specialist course. It originated in the West, has now spread widely, and is currently flourishing in China as well. Conferences and workshops on teaching critical thinking appear every year. Yet the course itself has no single, standardized content or method. As Stephen Brookfield notes, critical thinking draws on at least five major sources: analytic philosophy and logic, natural science, pragmatism, psychoanalysis, and critical theory.[^19] Teachers trained in different backgrounds will naturally emphasize different things. An ideally balanced course would make room for all five, but in practice that is difficult to achieve.

Second, critical thinking is also a more basic habit of mind. It runs through science, politics, and ordinary life. In principle, one aim of both school education and university education should be to strengthen this habit. Brookfield's own definition is still a useful starting point: critical thinking involves identifying the assumptions that shape our thought and action, checking how accurate and reliable those assumptions are, viewing our beliefs and decisions from multiple perspectives, and then acting more wisely on that basis.[^20]

This is why assumptions matter so much. Unlike approaches heavily shaped by formal logic, which often focus only on explicit premises, I want to include the wider background from which a judgment emerges. Brookfield distinguishes between normative, causal, and paradigmatic assumptions.[^21] Normative assumptions tell us what should count as the right move in a given situation. Causal assumptions tell us what follows from what. Paradigmatic assumptions are the hardest to see, because they shape what the facts look like in the first place. Wittgenstein's duck-rabbit image remains a standard example.[^22] Some people see a rabbit. Others see a duck. The visual data are the same, but the interpretive framework is not.

Something similar happens in social life. Consider Pope Francis's reaction when a woman suddenly grabbed and pulled him toward her during a New Year's greeting. One interpretation says that a moral leader briefly lost patience. Another says that an elderly man was nearly pulled over and reacted exactly as an ordinary person might. The crucial point is not to declare one reading finally victorious. It is to notice that different paradigmatic assumptions generate different moral interpretations of the same event. Francis's later response was revealing for precisely this reason: he did not simply apologize to the woman in a personal sense; rather, he apologized for setting a poor moral example. In other words, he distinguished between himself as an elderly man in a concrete situation and himself as a global religious symbol. That capacity to move across frameworks is close to what care education needs.

### Critical Thinking and Care Education

Although Brookfield's framework is highly useful, care education is not simply a critical thinking course under another name. It borrows some of critical thinking's methods and teaching ideals, but it often works through concrete material, such as environmental care, animal welfare, or life-care education more broadly. Its goals are also somewhat different. Critical thinking, by itself, is often very good at exposing assumptions, marking differences, and sharpening disagreement. Care education must do something more difficult. It has to make those differences visible without turning the classroom into a permanent scene of conflict.

That is why I sometimes say, only half-jokingly, that care education is a kind of "muddling through on the basis of precise analysis." What I mean is not intellectual laziness. I mean that the educator must preserve enough conceptual clarity to see where the disagreement lies, while also leaving enough room for the other side's standpoint to remain intelligible. In my view, that willingness to preserve space for the other person's position, without sacrificing basic cognitive honesty, is itself a form of social good will.

At the same time, care education demands an even broader form of discernment than ordinary critical thinking courses usually do. Social psychology reminds us that behaviour is shaped not only by explicit attitudes but also by implicit attitudes and peripheral cues. Myers distinguishes between a central route of persuasion, which works through reasons and evidence, and a peripheral route, which works through cues that people accept without much reflection.[^23] [^24] In the present age, saturated by social media, algorithmic targeting, and AI-generated content, the peripheral route is everywhere. That means care education cannot stop at the level of argument analysis. It must also involve some training in the self-management of cognition. But that training must itself remain scientifically informed and critically supervised.

### Teaching Principles

Several teaching principles follow from this.

1. Personal example matters. One important way to earn students' trust is through lived credibility. This trust comes partly from competence, but also from relation. Brookfield openly discusses in class how critical thinking helped him work through depression. In the same spirit, the cases I use in this chapter are real cases from my own experience. When we ask students to manage their own cognition more consciously, we ought to experiment on ourselves first: how we regulate online input, how we review our information channels, how we build mechanisms of balance and correction.

2. Social learning matters. This point can hardly be overstated. Group work remains one of the most useful teaching forms because it helps students see that different assumptions can underlie the same issue. One method I often like is a modified chalk-talk exercise. I give a controversial question to two groups, assign each a position, write the question in the centre of the board, and ask each group to place short key terms on either side while responding to one another. The advantage is that students can see, very directly, how different assumptions and concepts cluster around the same question.

3. Teaching must proceed gradually and with attention to difference. Students differ by age, gender, cultural background, confidence, and prior training. Teachers need to adjust materials and grouping arrangements accordingly, so that discussion does not collapse into domination by the strongest voices. Tools such as Brookfield's Critical Incident Questionnaire can help teachers test the accuracy of their own assumptions about what students are actually experiencing in the classroom.[^25]

4. Reading and writing remain indispensable. Well-chosen texts provide some of the clearest examples of sustained argument, and asking students to produce a relatively systematic essay remains one of the best ways to test the depth, structure, and stability of their thinking.

## Positive Psychology and Caring Practice

Someone might think that the relatively cautious account of care education I defend has little to do with anything "positive." I think that would be a mistake. Refusing to promise direct value-enhancement does not commit us to a negative psychology. Positive psychology still matters because any moral education worthy of the name must eventually lead to positive action, not merely to the possession of correct attitudes.

For care education, the positive element refers above all to patience in building relationships and to the cultivation of a healthier mode of life. Any caring relation is, in part, also a process of self-construction. Through relation, people learn both how they differ from others and how they themselves become identifiable. In ordinary life, two obstacles often interfere with this process.

The first obstacle often appears in collectivist settings. Some students strongly feel that they are different, do not fit in, and have been treated unfairly. Yet they still remain inside an active network of relations. They may fail to achieve consensus with others, but they are not simply invisible. In cases like this, teachers do not necessarily need to fear severe negative emotion. Often what is needed is an objective but positive evaluation of the student's assumptions and stance. Such a person has not been cut off from relation. Rather, the person struggles to establish care or consensus in a particular way. The surrounding network may refuse agreement, but it has not yet withdrawn recognition.

The second obstacle is more serious. Here the person cannot establish effective relations with others at all. That weakens confidence and deprives self-formation of sustained motivation. People in this condition are often highly sensitive both to themselves and to their relational environment, and in some cases they may also see their situation in a comparatively detached way. This is where depressive realism, or the "sadder but wiser" effect, becomes relevant.[^26] [^27] Ordinary people often exaggerate their control over events; depressed subjects often lack that optimistic distortion. Their characteristic style of explanation tends to treat failure as stable, pervasive, and internal.

This is why care education needs what I would call positive patience: confidence that the work of building relation can be effective. Such confidence can gradually become trust in oneself and, under some conditions, trust in others. Many factors shape this process, and some of them lie beyond the educator's control. Teachers therefore need to recognize their own limits and work carefully with the limited resources they actually possess.

The psychology here is complicated. In some circumstances, what sustains a person's positive orientation may look quite close to self-deception. Yet we do not always judge those mechanisms negatively. Brodt and Zimbardo's experiment on shyness is suggestive. Two groups of shy female students were asked to converse with the same attractive male experimenter. One group was first exposed to loud noise and told that bodily reactions such as increased heart rate were caused by the noise rather than by anxiety. The other group received no such framing. The students who had heard the noise appeared less shy.[^28] Is this self-deception in the strict sense? Perhaps in some respects it is close. But even if it is, some kinds of intervention may still be positively useful.

In my own philosophical work on self-deception, drawing on Alfred Mele, I have argued that cognitive bias can arise from both external and internal sources. Not every bias shaped by subjective attitude is therefore a moral defect. Some biases are unavoidable consequences of the paradigmatic assumptions through which each person already interprets the world. The key question is whether one can bring those assumptions under reflective scrutiny and ask whether they support or damage both relation and self-formation. That is difficult, but it cannot be avoided. For that reason, care education must combine cautious critical thinking with a refusal to drift toward depressive realism.[^29]

Positive psychology also matters in a practical sense. We should be cautious about the large number of self-improvement and educational interventions now on the market, many of which are ineffective or even harmful. Still, some modest practices are worth taking seriously: regular exercise, adequate sleep, and appropriate physical affection within close relationships, for example. Such practices can help support self-discipline. At the broadest level, this gives us a practical scale for care education: first, classroom teaching centred on critical thinking and knowledge; second, the broader school and family environment, where limited cognitive and behavioural interventions may be appropriate; and third, service learning, which takes students out into the community. These levels must develop progressively, not in reverse.

## Care and the Four Quotients

If the "four quotients" introduced earlier in the book are understood as four capacities, then care does not occupy exactly the same conceptual space. Care is both a capacity and an attitude toward life. As a capacity, it contains strong internal tensions. Those tensions may be moderated only when care is also lived as an orientation, not merely exercised as a skill.

Let us begin with care as a capacity. In that sense, it is intimately connected with all four quotients. Intelligence provides the cognitive condition for good care: if we cannot tell what the facts are, who most needs care, or how far care can be effective, then our caring intention may easily backfire. The Luo Yixiao case discussed earlier is a good example. She was certainly a proper object of concern, but national-scale pity directed overwhelmingly toward one case is itself difficult for any person to bear. Care requires not only feeling but judgment about object, proportion, and likely effect.

Emotional intelligence, in turn, is the carrier of care. It supplies the motive force, the psychological energy, and the felt reason for acting. Merely knowing who ought to be cared for and what that person needs does not yet guarantee caring action. This is one version of the problem Aristotle identifies in the practical syllogism: a person may possess the relevant conditions for action and still fail to act. Empathy supplies the will or meaning that allows care to become practice rather than remain an inert conclusion.

Moral intelligence concerns the social realization of care. People often connect care with moral qualities such as social responsibility. That is right, but social responsibility and care are not quite the same thing. Social responsibility is more specific; care is more general. To use a perhaps slightly playful analogy, care is like inner force in martial-arts fiction, while social responsibility is like the outward form of a technique. Inner force without technique does not make one a master, but technique without inner force also fails at decisive moments.

This difference matters because care cannot remain merely in the mind. It has to be realized through a concrete moral environment. One way to see this is through science fiction. Cixin Liu's *Three-Body* novels imagine that interstellar civilizations cannot establish relations of empathy, and that even morally advanced civilizations may therefore mistrust and destroy one another in a dark forest. If empathy were something that did not need to be socially realized, this problem would not arise. But because empathy must take institutional, cultural, and moral form, different worlds may embody it differently. Frank Herbert's *Dune* gives a vivid image of the same point: an act that counts as respect in one culture may appear offensive in another unless some mediating understanding is present. Care without social embodiment remains an unrealized mental possibility.

Finally, systems intelligence points toward the horizon within which these tensions can be held together. When we speak of a system, we imply that there are disharmonies within a larger whole that require coordination. The most difficult theoretical issue, in my view, lies here. Care itself contains irreducible tensions. My preference for second-person empathy already implies one of them. Second-person empathy is meant to avoid the defects of both first-person sympathy and third-person calculation, but no complete positive theory can fully reconcile the two. We know that emotion may be misled. We also know that pure effective altruist rationality is impossible to realize in its ideal form, and may even become counterproductive. Yet no final formula tells us exactly how to combine the strengths of both.

The same problem appears in other forms. Should empathy education stress embodiment or mentality? Embodied sensitivity matters, because many forms of relation begin at the level of perception and bodily response. Yet embodiment is limited by social and biological conditions. If you teach a child how to interact with a kitten, for example, you have to explain that a kitten may hug with its paws and kick with its hind legs in ways that seem rough, because that is part of its embodied expressive repertoire. One cannot simply abolish that fact. Mentalist approaches, by contrast, emphasize rational control and interpretation, but they too have limits. Reason has blind spots, and human beings never cease to be embodied creatures.

Similar tensions appear elsewhere: between specialist education and liberal education, between belief and will, and between intervention and restraint. Enhancement-oriented education tries to cultivate knowledge and skill within one framework. Liberal education asks for wider tolerance across frameworks. The two do not easily coincide. Likewise, a person may judge that something should be done and still fail to do it; or may accept the evidence intellectually while resisting it emotionally, producing some form of self-deception.

Systems thinking offers one possible response, but only if we understand it carefully. Earlier in the book, Zhang Yuanyuan describes what I would call a positive form of systems thinking, one that asks individuals or groups to step outside narrow perspectives and seek possibilities from a wider standpoint. In many global crises this way of thinking is indeed necessary. Yet its efficacy is still limited. Any system, once operationalized, becomes finite. Any perspective that claims to be complete eventually reveals its incompleteness.

So I want to add a further idea: a negative form of systems thinking, understood not as passivity but as restraint. Here the relevant attitude is close to what classical Chinese thought sometimes calls *wuwei*: not doing nothing, but refusing unnecessary and impulsive intervention. In many situations, doing less, looking longer, and acting only where action is necessary may be the wiser course. This kind of restraint sets a very high standard of self-regulation.

From the side of intelligence, that means monitoring not only what one thinks but also what one allows oneself to take in. In an age saturated by data and digital bombardment, every person needs some ability to examine the informational channels through which identity and judgment are formed. From the side of embodiment, it means recognizing how urban life and the loss of contact with nature weaken perceptual capacities that care depends upon. Practices that restore embodied alertness therefore matter.

This is where I want to end. At best, we can try to keep the conditions of care from becoming worse. We may be able to help learners remain above a humane baseline from which better relations remain possible. But education cannot guarantee excellence in any absolute sense. Too many factors remain uncertain. If that sounds somewhat anti-utopian, I am willing to accept the charge. I believe it is the more realistic and more responsible educational philosophy.
